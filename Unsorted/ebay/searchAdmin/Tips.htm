Tips for Searching

Quotation Marks
To find a title with words in an exact order, use quotation marks. For example, by typing: "brown bear" you will find title descriptions which include the exact phrase "brown bear".

And
To find a title with two or more words, use the term "and". For example, by typing: brown and bear you will get title descriptions with both the words "brown" and "bear" anywhere in the title.

Or
To find an item title that contains one of several words, use the term "or". For example, by typing: bear or brown you will find item titles with either the words "brown" or "bear."

And Not
To find an item description that contains one word and excludes another word, use the term "and not." For example, by typing: bear and not brown you will yield item titles which include the word "bear" and exclude the word "brown."

Sorting Your Results

Use the sort menu to select the order in which your results will be displayed.

Ending Date
Search will list your results according to the times that the auctions close

Bid Price
Search will list your results according to the price of the highest bid.

Search Ranking
Search will list your results according to how well items' titles match your search criteria.

And another tip on sort. The sooner you hit the resort of your choice (ending time, # of bids etc.), the more likely you'll catch your results while they're still cached for a quicker turn around.

Narrowing your search

You'll get the fastest response when the search query returns the fewest possible matches. For example, if you're looking for a particular "barbie" item, don't just type "barbie" in the query box. This will return a gazillion matches which will have to be sorted, and this takes time. We also have decided to not return *all* possible matches to you since it turns the system to goo. So it won't do you a whole lot of good anyway.

If you are looking for a barbie dress that was made in the 1960's, the best thing to type in would be "barbie and dress and 196*". The trick is to be specific! Lots of narrowly defined queries will get you a lot farther faster than one huge "give me the world" one.
